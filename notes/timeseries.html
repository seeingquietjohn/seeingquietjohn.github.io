Introduction

We consider a time series \(f(t)\) that is in fact \[f(t) = h(F(t)).\] In other words, \(f(t)\) is an observed time series of some hidden (and possibly much more complicated) time series \(F(t) : M \to N\). For simplicity, we will assume \(f(t)\) is one-dimensional and the observation function \(h\) is some one dimensional "projection" \(h: N \to \mathbb{R}\). Usually the goal here is to "reconstruct" \(F\) from \(f\). We will discuss any relevant ideas, interpretation, and problems that occur in this extremely general setting.

The "Simple" Case of Periodic Functions

Generated by a Dynamical System

In this section we work with time series generated by a dynamical system \(F:\mathbb{R}^m\to\mathbb{R}^n\), i.e., \[x_t = F_\mu (x_0)\] where \(x_0\) denotes the initial condition and \(\mu\) is some system parameter.

As usual, we try to understand the long-term asymptotic behavior of \(F_\mu\) across parameter values \(\mu\). We often assume that there exists some parameter value \(\mu_0\) for which the behavior of the corresponding system is well-understood. (Compare Examples of Simple Chaos.) However, as \(\mu\) is varied, the asymptotic behavior of \(F_\mu\) sometimes goes through a sudden drastic change, called a bifurcation. The parameter values at which such bifucations occur are called bifurcation points.
