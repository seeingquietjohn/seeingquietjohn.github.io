What is an Attractor?

Introduction

The attractor of a dynamical system \(f: M \to M\) plays an extremely important role in characterizing the long-term behavior of \(f\). Here we introduce the definition and a few examples of (strange) attractors.

Attractor of a Dynamical System

Given dynamical systems generated by some map \(f_\mu: M \to M\), we first note that its post-transient phase space, that is, the set of all possible points in \(M\) visited by the iterates \(f^t\) after a sufficiently long time \(T\), usually contains lots of helpful information about the dynamics. Informally speaking, an attractor of a dynamical system \(f_\mu: M\to M\) is the set of points in \(M\) to which most points evolve to under successive iterates of \(f\). Therefore, attractors of dynamical systems play an extremely important role in characterizing the dynamics qualitatively in various settings. We first review the notion of an attractor.

Attracting Sets

Let \(f: M\to M\). We say that a closed subset \(A \subset M\) is an attracing set if \(f^t(A) \subset A\) and it has a neighborhood \(U\) such that \(f^t(U) \subset V\) for sufficiently large \(t\) for every neighborhood \(V\) of \(A\).
