Examples of Bifurcations

Introduction

We provide examples and classification of bifurcations in differentiable dynamics on differentiable manifolds.

Differentiable Dynamical Systems

The time evolution of natural systems is usually given by a differential equation of the form \[\frac{dx}{dt} = X(x)\] where \(x = x(t)\) defines a smooth curve on some manifold \(M\). Here \(M\) is called a phase space as it contains all possible states for \(x\). In general, we are interested in the time evolution of \(x(t)\) as \(f: M \to TM\) defines a flow \(f: D \subset \mathbb{R} \times M \to M\) such that \[\frac{d}{dt}\phi(x, t) = X(f^t(x)).\] Note that \(f(x, t) = f^t(x)\) satisfies the group properties \(f^{t+s} = f^t \circ f^s\) and \(f^0 = \text{id}_M\) since \(f^0(x) = x\). Then the flow \(f^t\) on \(t \geq 0\) defines a continuous dynamical system; if \(t\) is restricted to integer values it becomes a discrete dynamical system.

To solve a differential equation we are often given some initial condition \[x(0) = x_0 \in M\] which defines a solution curve \(f^t(x_0)\) such that \[f(x_0, 0) = x_0.\] The existence and uniqueness of such a solution is guaranteed by the Picard-Lindelof theorem. Note that we can always assume \(t_0 = 0\) by the invariance of the vector field with respect to time. In the discussions below we shall see how the parametrized family of maps \(f_\mu\) behaves qualititavely as we vary \(\mu\).

Linear Systems

As a motivating example, we begin with a review of linear dynamical systems \[\frac{dx}{dt} = Ax.\]

Normal Growth and Decay

Consider the equation \[\frac{dx}{dt} = ax(t).\] Then for real \(k\), the solution to the equation is given by the family of curves \(x(t) = ke^{at}\) since \[\frac{dx}{dt} = ake^{at} = ax(t).\] If we fix an initial value \(x_0 = u_0\) we obtain a solution \[x(t) = u_0e^{at}\] based at \(x_0 = u_0\), which is unique (Picard-Lindelof).

Notice that if \(u_0 = 0\), then \(x(t)\) ends up being "stuck" at \(0\) for all \(t\). In general, given some differential equation of the form \(\dot{x} = f(x, t)\), the points \(x'\) at which \(\dot{x}\) vanishes are called equilibrium points (or fixed points); if \(q\) is an equilibrium point then \(x(t) \equiv q\) is the (constant) equilibrium solution.

Now we turn our attention to the paramter \(a\). As briefly discussed above, if \(a\neq 0\), then the constant map \(x(t) = 0\) is the equilibrium solution. More specifically, if \(a > 0\), then \(\lim_{t\to\infty}ake^{at} = \pm\infty\) depending on the sign of \(k\). On the other hand, if \(a < 0\), then \(\lim_{t\to\infty}ake^{at} = 0\). The equilibrium point \(0\) is called a source in the former, where the flow \(x(t)\) tends away from \(0\) as \(t\to\infty\). In the latter, \(0\) is called a sink as \(x(t)\) tends towards \(0\) as \(t\to\infty\). (Compare definitions of repellling and attracting fixed points.)
