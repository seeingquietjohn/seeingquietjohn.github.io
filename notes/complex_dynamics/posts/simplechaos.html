Introduction

We will describe several examples of "simple" systems of iterated functions to illustrate the definition of chaos in dynamics. By "simple" dynamics, we mean the kind of dynamics that is easily completely understood. That is, given a system \(f\), we can easily determine and describe the long-term behavior of the orbit \(\{f^n(x)\}\) under the map \(f\) for any input \(x\), where \(f^k(x)\) denotes the \(k\)-th iterate of \(f\).

Thus, for a holomorphic map \(f:\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\to\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\), we may say that the dynamics of \(f\) is simple if the dynamical dichotomy of the Riemann sphere into the Fatou set \(F(f)\) and the Julia set \(J(f)\) is topologically simple. It turns out that most Julia sets are complicated fractal subsets of \(\widehat\{\mathbb{C}}\), and there are only a few exceptions (up to automorphism of \(\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\)): the closed interval \([-2, 2]\), the unit circle \(S^1\), or the entire sphere \(\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\).
