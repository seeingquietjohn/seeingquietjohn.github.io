Fatou and Julia: Chaos on the Riemann Sphere

← Back to Complex Dynamics

Introduction

Informally speaking, dynamics is the study of patterns evolving under some rule in some space. That is, given some function \( f: A \to B \), we are interested in the long-term behaviors of its orbits \[ \{f(x), f(f(x)), f(f(f(x))), \dots \} \] for \( x \in A\). We will denote by \(f^k\) the \(k\)-fold iterate of \(f\).

In complex dynamics, we formalize such phenomena by studying the iterates of analytic maps \( f \) on a complex manifold. Here we briefly introduce some of the main ideas in complex dynamics.

Dynamical Decomposition of the Riemann Sphere

As a simple example, consider \( s(z) = z^2 \) on the Riemann sphere \( \widehat{\mathbb{C}} \). We see that the forward iterates of \( s \) inside the open disk \( \mathbb{D} = \{ z : |z| < 1 \} \) converge locally uniformly to zero. Similarly, any iterate of \( s \) converges locally uniformly to the constant map \(z \mapsto \infty\) on \(\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\setminus\overline{\mathbb{D}} = \{ z : |z| > 1 \}\). On the other hand, any forward orbit of \(s\) on the unit circle \( \partial \mathbb{D} = \{ z : |z| = 1 \} \) is "stuck" on the unit circle and any limit of iterates of \(s\) has a jump discontinuity as we "cross" the unit circle. (Compare the discussion of the angle doubling map in Simple Examples of Chaos and Smooth Julia Sets.)

We can thus split the Riemann sphere into two disjoint dynamically distinct regions \( \{z : |z| \neq 1\}\) where the dynamics of \(s\) is "tame", and \(\{z : |z| = 1\}\) on which the behavior of the iterates is "chaotic", or equivalently, displays sensitive dependence on initial conditions. Such a dichotomy of the Riemann sphere is generalized as the following.

Let \(U\) be an open subset of \(\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\) and \(\mathcal{F} = \{f_i\}\) be a family of functions \(f_i:U\to\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\). Then the family \(\mathcal{F}\) is normal on \(U\) if any infinite sequence of functions in \(\mathcal{F}\) contains a subsequence of functions that converges locally uniformly to some function \(g:U\to\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\). The domain of normality for the family of iterates \(\{f^k\}\) is called the Fatou set \(F_f\), and its complement the Julia set \(J_f = \widehat{\mathbb{C}} \setminus F_f\).

Thus, a point \(z \in \widehat{\mathbb{C}}\) belongs to the Fatou set if there exists an open neighborhood \(U\) of \(z\) such that the collection \(\{f^k |_U\}\) forms a such that the collection \(\{f^k |_U\}\) forms a normal family. Otherwise, a small perturbation of the initial point \(z\) on any of its neighborhood \(U\) leads to a drastically different long-term behavior and \(z\) belongs to the Julia set. (See Entropy and Dimension.)

Briefly going back to our example \(s(z) = z^2\), it follows that the Julia set \(J_s\) is precisely the unit circle \(\{ z : |z| = 1 \}\). In fact, it is not too difficult to see that the Julia set of the power map \(p_d(z) = z^d\) is the unit circle for any integer \(d \geq 2\). As we will see later, it turns out that such smooth Julia sets are quite rare and most Julia sets exhibit complex fractal structures. (See Simple Examples of Chaos and Smooth Julia Sets.)

Properties of the Julia Set

We now discuss some of the basic properties of the Julia set for a nonconstant holomorphic map on the Riemann sphere. In this section we assume \(f:\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\to\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\) is a nonconstant analytic map.

Invariance Lemma. The Julia set \(J_f\) is totally invariant. That is, \[f^{-1}(J_f) = J_f = f(J_f).\] Equivalently, the Fatou set \(F_f\) is totally invariant.

Iteration Lemma. For any \(k > 0\), \(J(f^k) = J(f)\).

Now we take a closer look at the analytical properties of the Julia set. Consider a periodic orbit or cycle \[z_0 \mapsto z_1 \mapsto \dots \mapsto z_{n-1} \mapsto z_0\] for a holomorphic map \(f\) of period \(n\). The first derivative of the \(n\)-fold iterate \(f^n\) at \(z_0\) \[\lambda = (f^n)'(z_0)\] is called the multiplier of the periodic orbit. The orbit is attracting if \(\lvert\lambda\rvert < 1\), indifferent if \(\lvert\lambda\rvert = 1\), and repelling if \(\lvert\lambda\rvert > 1\). These defintions can be easily verified visually by graphical analyses of orbits. An attracting orbit is superattracting if \(\lambda = 0\) and geometrically attracting if \(0 < |\lambda| < 1\). For example, the point at infinity \(\infty\) and \(0\) are two superattracting fixed points for the power map \(p_d(z) = z^d\). (Compare Polynomial Julia Sets.)

Furthermore, attracting orbits \(\mathcal{O}\) are equipped with an open set of points that eventually converge to \(\mathcal{O}\) under iteration, called the basin of attraction. The open disk \(\mathbb{D}\) and its exterior are attracting basins for \(0\) and \(\infty\) for the power map, respectively. In fact, we saw that these two regions make up the entire Fatou set \(F_{p_d}\), while the Julia set \(J_{p_d}\) contained the repelling periodic points \[\{e^{i\theta} : \theta = \frac{2\pi m}{d^n-1}, \ n \geq 1, 0 \leq m \leq d^n-1\}.\]

Attracing Basins and Repelling Points. Every attracting basin \(\mathcal{A}\) of a holomorphic map \(f:\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\to\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\) is contained in the Fatou set \(F_f\). On the other hand, every repelling periodic orbit is contained in the Julia set \(J_f\).
Proof. Since a periodic point \(z = f^n(z)\) is a fixed point of \(f^n\), by the iteration lemma above, it suffices to prove the statement for fixed points. Let \(z_0 = f(z_0)\) be a fixed point of \(f\) with multiplier \(\lambda = f'(z_0)\). If \(\lambda > 1\), then no sequence can converge uniformly around \(z_0\) by the Weierstrass uniform convergence theorem. On the other hand, suppose \(|\lambda| < 1\). We can indeed work with a local chart with \(z_0 = 0\).
