Introduction

Informally speaking, dynamics is the study of patterns evolving under some rule in some space. That is, given some function \( f \), we are interested in the long-term behaviors of its orbits \[ \{f(x), f(f(x)), f(f(f(x))), \dots \} \] for input values \( x \). We will denote by \(f^k\) the \(k\)-fold iterate of \(f\).

Complex dynamics is a mathematical formalization of such phenomena. It is the study of the iterates of analytic maps \( f \) on a complex manifold. Here we briefly introduce some of the main ideas in complex dynamics.

Dynamical Decomposition of the Riemann Sphere

As a simple example, consider \( s(z) = z^2 \) on the Riemann sphere \( \widehat{\mathbb{C}} \). We see that the forward iterates of \( s \) inside the open disk \( \mathbb{D} = \{ z : |z| < 1 \} \) converge locally uniformly to zero. Similarly, any iterate of \( s \) converges locally uniformly to the constant map \(z \mapsto \infty\) on \(\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\setminus\overline{\mathbb{D}} = \{ z : |z| > 1 \}\). On the other hand, any forward orbit of \(s\) on the unit circle \( \partial \mathbb{D} = \{ z : |z| = 1 \} \) is "stuck" on the unit circle and any limit of iterates of \(s\) has a jump discontinuity as we "cross" the unit circle.

Intuitively, we can describe the dynamics of \( s \) on the open disk \( \mathbb{D} \) and its exterior \( \widehat{\mathbb{C}}\setminus\overline{\mathbb{D}} \) as "tame", while the dynamics on the unit circle \( \partial \mathbb{D} \) is "chaotic", or equivalently, displays sensitive dependence on initial conditions. In general, for a nonconstant holomorphic function \(f\), we can partition the Riemann sphere into two disjoint sets based on the dynamical behavior of \(z\in\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\) under iteration of \(f\). Namely, the Fatou set \(F(f)\) of \(f\) is the set of points \(z\) whose dynamics is tame, and the Julia set \(J(f)\) is the collection of points whose dynamics is chaotic.

More formally, let \(U\) be an open subset of \(\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\) and let \(\mathcal{F} = \{f_i\}\) be a family of functions \(f_i:U\to\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\). We say that \(\mathcal{F}\) is normal if any infinite sequence of functions in \(\mathcal{F}\) contains a subsequence of functions that converges locally uniformly to some function \(g:U\to\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\). Then the Fatou set is the domain of normality for the family of iterates \(\{f^k\}\), and the Julia set is its complement on \(\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\).

Thus, a point \(z \in \widehat{\mathbb{C}}\) belongs to the Fatou set if there exists an open neighborhood \(U\) of \(z\) such that the collection \(\{f^k |_U\}\) forms a normal family. Otherwise, \(z\) belongs to the Julia set and the neighborhood \(U\) is a domain of sensitive dependence on initial conditions, that is, a small perturbation of the initial point \(z\) leads to a drastically different long-term behavior. (See topological entropy.)

Briefly going back to our example \(s(z) = z^2\), it follows that the Julia set \(J(s)\) is precisely the unit circle \(\{\ z : |z| = 1 \}\). In fact, it is not too difficult to see that the Julia set of the power map \(p_d(z) = z^d\) is the unit circle for any integer \(d \geq 2\). As we will see later, it turns out that such smooth Julia sets are quite rare and most Julia sets exhibit complex fractal structures. (See Simple Examples of Chaos and Smooth Julia Sets.)
